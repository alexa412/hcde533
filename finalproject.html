Final Project

by Alexa Schlein for HCDE 533, December 2022

Bees, Flowers, and Honeycomb Jewelry Set

Concept

My final project focuses on a bee- and honeycomb-themed jewelry set with a matching jewelry dish. In the spirit of limiting plastic waste, I used leftover acrylic laser cut flowers from my lamp project. Here's what I made!

I created:

  • A pair of 3D printed + laser cut acrylic honeycomb earrings
  • A necklace made with a 3D printed bee + laser cut acrylic flowers
  • 3D printed honeycomb rings
  • A 3D printed + laser cut acrylic honeycomb jewelry dish

Techniques

I used the following techniques in my project:

  • Rhino (modeling and light mesh editing)
  • Grasshopper
  • 3D printing
  • Laser cutting
  • Incorporating stock parts

Bee & Flower Necklace

Design

I used the leftover acrylic laser cutter flowers from my lamp assignment. Please see this page for more details about how these pieces were designed and cut.

I based my bee design on this bee straw topper that I found on Etsy. I purchased the STL file and imported it into Rhino (mostly because I thought it would be faster and cleaner than designing my own bee). Because I wanted to make some changes (and this STL is for a topper, rather than the piece I need), I then took a screenshot of the STL in Rhino, pasted it into Illustrator, and built my own bee curves by tracing the picture. I imported those curves from Illustrator into Rhino and made additional curves and modifications to create the shape I wanted. I added hooks to the bee for attaching jump rings, but I decided to not use a necklace chain, and used my leftover acrylic chain pieces instead.

3D Printing

I 3D printed the honeycomb pieces with the following slicer settings:

  • Standard quality
  • 75% infill
  • 200 degrees F print temperature
  • 0.2mm layer height

Finishing & Assembly

I lightly sanded the bee with 320 grit sandpaper, and painted it using the metallic strawberry acrylic paint from my paint set. Originally, I was going to hook the bee to acrylic pieces and a necklace chain with jump rings, which is why I added the hooks. But, the necklace chains I bought didn't look right with the acrylic, so I used more leftover chain pieces to create a necklace chain. The "pendant" portion is made with acrylic flowers attached to the bee. I thought about adding some honeycomb pieces but... less is more, I think. Overall, it looks okay. I think it was a decent execution of an okay idea, haha.


Honeycomb Earrings

Design

I first created a basic honeycomb curve in Grasshopper following this tutorial. I baked that curve into Rhino and deleted honeycomb hexagon pieces to create a general shape I liked. Then, I added a hook curve, and extruded the curve to create a 3D honeycomb model for printing. I also exported the honeycomb curve to Adobe Illustrator to cut and engrave acrylic.

Creating a Honeycomb with Grasshopper

I used a hexagonal component to create a honeycomb wall shape, and created a scaled version where each scaled honeycomb piece is centered into the larger piece. See below:

3D Models in Rhino

The honeycomb pieces are 3.175mm (0.125in) thick (I baked the curve and extruded it in Rhino).

3D Printing

I printed the honeycomb pieces with the following slicer settings:

  • Standard quality
  • 75% infill
  • 0.2mm layer height

I just flipped the STL around in Cura for the opposite side.

Laser Cutting

Using my leftover fluorescent yellow acrylic from the lamp project, I laser cut and engraved a honeycomb shape using the cell cast acrylic material settings for laser cutter #2 in the 8 makerspace. The engraved honeycomb is slightly smaller in scale than the 3D printed honeycomb. Unfortunately, I had some issues with aligning the laser cutter, so I had to do multiple cuts. I also had to sand my pieces after cutting because they didn't cut cleanly through. For next time, I'd advise tinkering power settings to get a clean cut.

Finishing & Assembly

First, I lightly sanded the 3D printed honeycomb and then painted it with metallic gold acrylic paint. Then, I sanded the laser cut honeycomb and superglued them to the 3D printed honeycomb. I then attached a earring hook and jump ring to the earring.



Honeycomb Rings

Design

So, I actually designed a few different rings because I had the wrong honeycomb orientation! Regardless, my ring design was built in Grasshopper and 3D printed. The rings fit my fingers.

Creating a Honeycomb with Grasshopper

The ring was designed using a combination of the honeycomb earring Grasshopper components + the Grasshopper flow command I used to build my lamp base for the lamp assignment. I'll outline the portions below:

Step 1: Create a honeycomb curve structure.

Step 2: Create a scaled version of that honeycomb curve structure, where each smaller honeycomb hexagon is centered within the larger honeycomb hexagon.

Step 3: Extrude the honeycomb structure.

Step 4: Flow the honeycomb structure onto a circle set to the ring diameter.

NOTE: For some reason, unless I had the number of honeycomb set very high, the honeycomb hexagons would stretch. So, you do have to delete leftover pieces. I'm sure there's something I could be doing better here, but this works for now.

I actually designed 2 rings because I made a mistake with the first design (incorrect honeycomb orientation). See below! I did this by simply flipping the x and y values in the hexagon component in my Grasshopper definition.

3D Modeling in Rhino

For design 1 (incorrect honeycomb orientation), I baked the ring into Rhino from Grasshopper and deleted the leftover pieces. For design 2 (correct honeycomb orientation), I baked the ring into Rhino, and then removed a portion of the top and bottom layers with the BooleanDifference command. I discovered this was necessary after having issues printing this design (the thickness of the flat part of the hexagon was too thin to print well).

The rings are open because of the deleted pieces from the Flow command

3D Printing

Several of my initial prints failed due to design flaws and slicer settings.

I had to do several iterations to figure out the right printing settings because I had problems with adhesion and general quality. If you do print from one of my STL files, I would recommend testing temperatures, speeds, infills, and top/bottom layer heights to find what works best for you. In general though, my slicer settings were about this:

  • Super quality
  • 75% infill
  • 195 degrees F print temperature
  • 0.12mm layer height
  • 0.20mm/s speed
  • Raft for adhesion

Finishing & Assembly

I did some light sanding, but honestly didn't have time to perfect it, so mostly I just made sure I cleared away any bumps or pieces sticking out. I painted some of the rings with acrylic paints!


Jewelry Dish

Design

I designed the bottom of my jewelry dish to be laser cut and assembled with super glue and a chicago screw. The dish bottom contains some of my leftover laser cut flowers. This dish bottom is designed to be glue to a honeycomb structure jewelry dish base.

Grasshopper

I used the same Grasshopper definition that I used for the honeycomb rings. But, I used the dish diameter instead of the ring size for the flow command. I also increased the width and thickness. In general, though, the code is similar!

3D Modeling in Rhino - Dish Base

The bottom of the base is a revolved L-shape. The middle of it is open to save material, and also allow room for the bottom of the Chicago screw. Once I baked the honeycomb structure into Rhino, I joined the honeycomb to the revolved portion. Similar to the honeycomb rings, I made multiple designs with different amounts of honeycomb and orientations.

3D Printing

I 3D printed the honeycomb dish bases with the following slicer settings:

  • Standard quality
  • 25% infill
  • 0.12mm layer height
  • 0.40mm/s speed
  • 200 degrees F print temperature
  • Brim for adhesion

3D Modeling - Dish Bottom

I designed the acrylic circles for the dish bottom in Rhino. They're 100mm in diameter and fit the Chicago screws I purchased.

Laser Cutting

I used the settings for cell cast acrylic in the materials database for laser cutter #2 in the 8. As I described previously, the flower pieces came from my lamp assignment.

Finishing & Assembly

I ended up not needing the smallest circle for the screw size I used. I super glued the white acrylic circle to the clear acrylic circle (O-shaped). I then placed laser cut acrylic flowers (from my lamp project) inside this. Then, I placed a clear acrylic circle on top and secured these pieces together with a Chicago screw. I used a Chicago screw (leather screw) because it allows motion and doesn't require threads.

Again, I did minimal sanding with sandpaper, just to remove pieces sticking out and to smooth sharp edges. I painted the accurate honeycomb dish base with a few metallic acrylic paints. I don't like the result though, so I haven't glued the laser cut bottom to any of the bases. I think I'm going to print a new one, leave it white, and finally glue the dish base to the dish bottom with super glue.


Deviations

Luckily, I managed to complete most everything that I had planned. But, I had to make the following changes to my original proposal:

  • Change in necklace chain (personal preference)
  • Minimal sanding (I ran out of time for my preferred finish)
  • No rendering (I ran out of time)
