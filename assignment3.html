Building My Printer

I have visitors from out of town, so I've been building my printer slowly in small sprints. It probably took me about 2 and a half hours in total, over a few days, to get the printer built. I did use Nadya's slide deck to make sure there were no cracks of light coming through intersecting parts on my printer base - so it ended up being a pain to get the screws just right. I got confused with setting up the y-axis, so I followed this YouTube tutorial. I saw a message from Junchao in Discord, and realized that my belt was reversed, so I had to fix that too. Leveling My Printer

OH BOY. I was so confused with leveling the printer. Like everyone else, I followed this YouTube tutorial (same one as the rest of the class). I accidentally tried to do the squares printing step first. During this, I realized that my binder clips were in the wrong place (they were on the sides instead of the front and back), so the nozzle scratched on the clips and on the glass surface itself. Whoops. I had to adjust the z-switch several times (following this YouTube video) until I found the right position.

Then, when I got back on the right track, it took me a few tries to level my bed. I was pretty lost with the adjustment screws, so I decided to tighten them to about the mid-level, and restart the leveling. I used a sticker for the leveling, and leveled the bed once more using a mailer from Ulta Beauty. Then, when I was FINALLY ready to do the leveling squares print, I wasn't sure how to insert my filament. So, I followed this video to do so. Then, FINALLY, I printed my leveling squares. Amazingly, they adhered perfectly the first time, and I didn't have to adjust my bed at all.

Building the Cube and Cylinder 3D Models

Building the cube and the cylinder were super easy in Rhino. I just extruded a square and a circle for the cube and the cylinder, respectively. The tubes, on the other hand…

The Tube Problem

It took me a while to find out about the tube command. Like, in SolidWorks, normally I would make a tube by extruding 2 circles (or extrude 1 circle and cut away a second, smaller circle). But, I found and used the Rhino Tube command. At first, I got confused with the dimensions and tried to make a tube with a 0.04mm wall thickness, which refused to slice in Cura. Once I got the dimensions sorted, well, my tube still refused to load in Cura (it appeared as a box). I followed this YouTube tutorial to convert a model in Rhino to a mesh object, and I exported the mesh to an STL. I repeated this process for the 0.08mm wall thickness tube. Finally, both of those were able to be sliced in Cura.

Nested Object - Turner's Cube

Inspiration

Years ago, at my first job out of college, one of my coworkers showed me a nested cube that he had 3d printed at home. I barely remembered what it looked like, but I knew it was a nested cube. After a few Google searches, I found what I was looking for: A Turner's Cube! (Source)

A Turner's Cube is a common project made with a CNC mill. It's a series of nest cubes with bores. Each cube can move around individually, and the inner cubes can't fall out of the structure, so it stays nested and assembled.

Building a 3D Model

I wasn't sure about the dimensions to use, so I followed this project's dimensions. This person used subtractive manufacturing to build a 2 inch Turner's Cube with 3 cubes. I built the smallest cube first in Rhino using the Rectangle, ExtrudeCrv, Circle, and MakeHole commands (again, I had to Google to find the right commands! Why doesn't Rhino work the same as SolidWorks! Haha). Then, I made a copy of the cube and scaled it 1.77x - just to the point where the smaller cube's width was about the same as the diameter of the bigger cube's bore. I repeated this process for the largest cube. Don't copy my process because it's not correct. There's actual math behind it that I didn't see until I started writing up my documentation. Test Prints

I exported my Turner's cube as an STL, and then found out it was going to take hours to print. I followed the print settings from this project. So, I decided to do test prints at ⅕ of the size. I wasn't sure what kinds of supports were needed (and Google wasn't helping), so I ran the first test cube with supports everywhere - that took about 14 minutes to print and I wasn't able to remove the supports. I ran my second test cube with supports that touch the build plate only. That took about 11 minutes to print. I still wasn't able to remove the supports, but the top half of the cube built successfully without supports everywhere, so I decided to go for it!

Final Prototyping

Using the full scale STL file, I printed my Turner's cube with 50% infill, concentric top/bottom pattern, low quality, and supports touching the build plate. The final print took 3 hours, 47 minutes, and 31 seconds. I was able to punch out some of the supports. For the supports on the bottom of the cube, I actually used a cheese board knife to (gently) break it off. Here's the final result!


If you shake it, it doesn't come apart! Yay nesting! Yay puzzles!

Reflection

I would've saved a lot of time had I gone to YouTube before and paid more attention when trying to build and level my printer. I'm also really happy I was able to build the Turner's cube. I do wish I had made the cube mathmatically accurate (whoops) and added fillets, but I can't complain because it works!
